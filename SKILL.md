# Effective Presentation Slides — Content & Structure

A slide deck is a communication device. Every decision — what goes on a slide, what the title says, how content is weighted across slides — should serve one goal: help the audience receive one clear message per slide, without cognitive overload.

> **Scope of this skill:** Content, structure, and narrative arc only. For visual design, typography, color, and stage-specific formatting, use `/ui-ux-pro-max` or `/frontend-design`.

---

## Narrative arc

Before structuring individual slides, the deck needs a dramatic arc. The brain stores knowledge as stories — narrative is not rhetorical decoration; it is the operating system of cognition. Data alone rarely changes behavior; a story with data inside it does.

### The audience is the hero — you are the mentor

This principle applies to every arc below. Never make yourself or your company the protagonist. The audience has the problem, carries the burden, and must make the decision. Your role is the mentor: you possess the knowledge to hand the audience the tool that solves *their* conflict.

- Every "We are the market leader" weakens the deck.
- Every "You fight with X every day — here is what changes that" strengthens it.

*Weak (speaker as hero):* "Our tool has the fastest pipeline on the market, built by top engineers."
*Strong (audience as hero):* "You spend every Friday afternoon on manual deployments and are the last ones to leave. Here is what changes that — press once, the rest runs."

### Step 1 — Ask before choosing an arc

When starting a new deck, ask the user these three questions before recommending a structure:

1. **Goal:** What should the audience *do or believe* after this talk?
   - Get a decision / approval
   - Understand something new
   - Feel motivated to change
   - Receive a status update
2. **Audience:** Who is in the room?
   - Executive decision-makers
   - Peers / colleagues
   - Customers / prospects
   - Conference / public audience
3. **Format:** What kind of presentation is this?
   - Pitch or proposal
   - Keynote or conference talk
   - Internal update or all-hands
   - Training or workshop

Based on the answers, recommend one arc from the options below. When the user already has a clear structure in mind, confirm it fits the goal rather than imposing a different one.

### Step 2 — Choose the right arc

**A. Five-act arc** — *Change proposal, internal pitch, strategic initiative*
Use when: You need to move decision-makers from the status quo to a new direction and must build emotional buy-in before presenting the solution.

| Act | Content |
|-----|---------|
| Exposition | Meet the audience where they are; establish shared context |
| Rising action | Why the status quo is unsustainable: costs, risks, consequences of inaction |
| Climax | The single core insight or new direction |
| Falling action | Implications, implementation, residual risks |
| Dénouement | One concrete call-to-action |

The most common failure: skipping rising action and jumping straight to the solution. Without built-up urgency, the solution feels arbitrary.

*Example — CRM rollout:* (1) "We manage customer data in three separate spreadsheets." (2) "This costs each account manager 2 hours/week — last month we lost a client because two teams sent conflicting offers." (3) "A central CRM solves this structurally." (4) "Migration in three phases, starting with Sales." (5) "Today we need a go-decision for the Q3 pilot."

---

**B. SCQA** — *Executive briefing, consulting recommendation, board update*
Use when: The audience already knows the context; they need the point fast. Skips scene-setting in favor of immediate tension.

| Step | Content |
|------|---------|
| Situation | What everyone already knows — brief, factual |
| Complication | What has changed or what is wrong |
| Question | The implied "so what do we do?" |
| Answer | Your recommendation or finding — stated upfront |

Slides after the answer provide the supporting evidence. This is a "bottom-line up front" structure.

*Example:* (S) "We currently onboard clients manually over 14 days." (C) "Two enterprise clients churned in Q2 citing slow setup." (Q) "How do we protect the remaining pipeline?" (A) "Automate onboarding to 3 days — here is how."

---

**C. Problem-Solution** — *Product pitch, sales presentation, feature proposal*
Use when: The audience has a felt pain point and you are offering a concrete solution. The problem must be vivid before the solution lands.

| Step | Content |
|------|---------|
| Problem | The pain, made specific and felt |
| Root cause | Why existing approaches fail |
| Solution | Your answer, shown not told |
| Proof | Evidence: data, case study, demo |
| Call to action | Single next step |

---

**D. Sparkline** — *Vision keynote, inspirational talk, culture change*
Use when: You want to inspire a shift by repeatedly contrasting painful present reality with an achievable future. No single climax — the oscillation *is* the argument.

Structure: Alternate "What is" and "What could be" throughout the entire deck, ending on the future state.

Sanity check — the Pixar chain: *"Every day X happened … until one day Y … and because of that Z … until finally …"* If you cannot complete this for your deck, the arc is missing.

*Example — invoicing tool:* What is: "Every day Lisa manually types invoices into Word and loses track of what has been paid." What could be: "With one click, Lisa creates, sends, and tracks every invoice — and sees live which ones are overdue." Pixar: *"Every day Lisa typed invoices manually … until one day a wrong quote went out … and because of that a major client left … until finally one button gave Lisa her Friday afternoons back."*

---

**E. Data-driven** — *Research presentation, analytical report, evidence briefing*
Use when: The audience expects evidence before conclusions. Credibility comes from showing the work; the recommendation must be earned.

| Step | Content |
|------|---------|
| Context | Why this question matters |
| Evidence | Data, findings, observations — shown, not summarized |
| Insight | What the evidence means |
| Implications | Consequences of the insight |
| Recommendation | What to do next |

---

**F. Journey / Chronological** — *Company retrospective, case study, founding story*
Use when: Time is the natural organizing principle and the narrative arc *is* the story itself.

| Step | Content |
|------|---------|
| Past | Where we started / what we faced |
| Turning point | What changed or what we learned |
| Present | Where we are now |
| Future | Where we are going / what we are asking for |

## 10 Rules for Slide Content & Structure

### Scope

**Rule 1 — One idea per slide.**
If a slide tries to deliver two messages, it delivers neither well. Split complex topics into a sequence and build up step by step. When unsure whether something belongs on a slide, ask: is this the *one* thing the audience should remember?

**Rule 2 — ~1 minute per slide.**
Use this as both a pacing check and a complexity check. A 20-minute talk → ~20 slides. When a slide needs more than a minute to explain, it almost always needs to be cut or split.

**Rule 3 — Titles state the conclusion.**
Titles are not labels ("Results"). They are the takeaway in one sentence: *"User errors drop 40 % with the new flow."* Write the conclusion first, then build the slide to support it. The audience should be able to read the key point from the title alone.

**Rule 4 — Only what will be spoken.**
Every element on a slide is an implicit promise to address it. Anything the speaker won't explicitly cover is a distraction. Cut it ruthlessly. If removing something wouldn't change what the audience learns, remove it.

### Content strategy

**Rule 5 — Show it, don't tell it.**
Don't assert a point in prose the audience must read while they listen — depict it, demonstrate it, or present the evidence so the point is *seen*, not *told*. Almost no slide should be text-only. Images paired with speech are recalled far better than words alone — roughly 65 % vs. 10 % after three days (Paivio, Medina *Brain Rules*).

Four concrete moves:

- **Claim → evidence:** If you'd say "errors dropped 40 %", show the before/after chart with the delta annotated on it.
- **Description → artifact:** Instead of describing a tool, design, or result, show it — a labelled screenshot or diagram carries more than a paragraph.
- **Concept → contrast or sequence:** Abstract ideas become concrete through side-by-side comparison, step-by-step storyboard, or before/after pairing.
- **Introduce before concluding:** Tell the audience what they are looking at before stating the point the visual makes.

When adding a visual, *replace* prose rather than stacking it alongside — combining both restores the cognitive load you were trying to remove (Mayer's redundancy principle).

**Rule 6 — Avoid cognitive overload.**
Reading and listening compete for the same cognitive channel. Dense on-slide text forces the audience to choose one or the other. Instead:

- Keep text **brief and reinforcing** — short cues that echo speech, not replace it.
- Limit the number of competing elements per slide. Less is more.
- Move detailed context to backup slides after the conclusion — available in Q&A but not interrupting the talk.

**Rule 7 — Understandable without the narration.**
If someone was distracted and heard nothing, would they still grasp the key point? The title + visual + brief labels should achieve this. Slides that only work when the speaker explains them are a liability.

### Attention & Memory

**Rule 8 — Lead with the core message, close with the call-to-action.**
Working memory is unloaded at the start (primacy effect) and still active at the end (recency effect). The middle of a talk is a cognitive dead zone. Put the most important claim in the first minutes — not as a dramatic climax saved for the end. Make the final slide a single, concrete action — not a summary dump.

**Rule 9 — Open a loop early, close it late.**
Start with an open question, an unexplained data point, or the beginning of a story — and deliberately withhold the resolution. The brain compulsively seeks closure (Zeigarnik effect), which keeps attention engaged through the middle section. The payoff at the end also amplifies the recency effect: the audience leaves with both the answer and the relief of closure.

**Rule 10 — Break the pattern once, deliberately.**
If the presentation runs consistently rational and data-driven, a single deliberate break — an unexpected image, a moment of vulnerability, a physical demonstration — registers as a cognitive isolator (Von-Restorff effect) and is remembered disproportionately. This only works when the surrounding content is homogeneous; in a chaotic deck the effect disappears.

## When reviewing or critiquing a deck

Check the narrative arc first:
- Who is the hero — the audience or the speaker? Does the deck solve the audience's problem?
- Can you complete the Pixar chain for this deck? If not, the arc is missing.
- Does the deck build urgency (rising action) before presenting the solution?
- Is the core message in the first few minutes (Rule 8), not buried in the middle?
- Does the deck open with an unresolved question or tension (Rule 9)?
- Is there one memorable break in the pattern (Rule 10)?

Then apply this lens per slide:
- What is the single message? Is it immediately clear?
- Does the title state the conclusion?
- Is the point *shown* (visual, chart, artifact, annotated example) or merely *told* (asserted in prose)?
- Does anything appear that the speaker probably won't address?
- Would the point survive without the narration?

Flag patterns across the deck:
- Slides consistently overloaded with multiple messages (Rule 1)
- Generic labels used as titles throughout (Rule 3)
- Slides that require the speech to make sense (Rule 7)
- Speaker positioned as hero rather than mentor (Narrative arc)
- Missing urgency — solution presented without established problem (Arc step 2)
