# Effective Presentation Slides

A slide deck is a communication device. Every decision — what goes on a slide, what the title says, how visual elements are weighted — should serve one goal: help the audience receive one clear message per slide, without cognitive overload.

The 10 rules below draw on presentation research and memory science. The citations are background; the rules are what matter.

---

## The 10 Rules

### Scope

**Rule 1 — One idea per slide.**
If a slide tries to deliver two messages, it delivers neither well. Split complex topics into a sequence and build up step by step. When unsure whether something belongs on a slide, ask: is this the *one* thing the audience should remember?

**Rule 2 — ~1 minute per slide.**
Use this as both a pacing check and a complexity check. A 20-minute talk → ~20 slides. When a slide needs more than a minute to explain, it almost always needs to be cut or split.

**Rule 3 — Titles state the conclusion.**
Titles are not labels ("Results"). They are the takeaway in one sentence: *"User errors drop 40 % with the new flow."* Write the conclusion first, then build the slide to support it. The audience should be able to read the key point from the title alone.

**Rule 4 — Only what will be spoken.**
Every element on a slide is an implicit promise to address it. Anything the speaker won't explicitly cover is a distraction. Cut it ruthlessly. If removing something wouldn't change what the audience learns, remove it.

**Rule 5 — Credit consistently, immediately.**
Add citations and contributor credits as the slide is built, not as a post-processing step. This prevents the common failure of presenting uncredited figures that need retroactive sourcing.

### Design

**Rule 6 — Show it, don't tell it.**
Don't assert a point in prose the audience must read while they listen — depict it, demonstrate it, or present the evidence so the point is *seen*, not *told*. Build every slide around a visualization; almost no slide should be text-only. Images paired with speech are recalled far better than words alone — roughly 65 % vs. 10 % after three days (Paivio, Medina *Brain Rules*).

Four concrete moves:

- **Claim → evidence:** If you'd say "errors dropped 40 %", show the before/after chart with the delta annotated on it.
- **Description → artifact:** Instead of describing a tool, design, or result, show it — a labelled screenshot or diagram carries more than a paragraph.
- **Concept → contrast or sequence:** Abstract ideas become concrete through side-by-side comparison, step-by-step storyboard, or before/after pairing.
- **Annotate the takeaway on the visual:** A short label, arrow, or callout placed precisely on the key element directs attention and doubles as a retention cue.

Introduce the figure's elements before stating the conclusion — tell the audience what they are looking at first. And when adding a visual, *replace* prose rather than stacking it alongside — combining both restores the cognitive load you were trying to remove.

**Rule 7 — Avoid cognitive overload.**
Reading and listening compete for the same cognitive channel. Dense on-slide text forces the audience to choose one or the other. Instead:

- Keep text **brief and reinforcing** — short cues that echo speech, not replace it.
- Limit the number of competing elements. Less is more.
- Make the element that carries the message **visually dominant** — the eye should go there immediately.
- Maintain a clear visual hierarchy: important things look important, supporting detail looks secondary.
- When adding a visual, *replace* prose rather than stacking text alongside it — combining both restores the cognitive load you were trying to remove (Mayer's redundancy principle).

**Rule 8 — Understandable without the narration.**
If someone was distracted and heard nothing, would they still grasp the key point? The title + visual + brief labels should achieve this. Slides that only work when the speaker explains them are a liability. Move detailed context slides to after the conclusion so they're available in Q&A but don't interrupt the talk.

## When reviewing or critiquing a deck

Apply this lens per slide:
- What is the single message? Is it immediately clear?
- Is the most important element visually dominant?
- Does the title state the conclusion?
- Does anything appear that the speaker probably won't address?
- Would the point survive without the narration?
- Is the point *shown* (visual, chart, artifact, annotated example) or merely *told* (asserted in prose)?

Flag patterns across the deck:
- Slides consistently overloaded (Rule 1 / Rule 7)
- Generic titles throughout (Rule 3)
- Visual hierarchies that bury the main point (Rule 7)
- Slides that require the speech to make sense (Rule 8)

## Stage presentations (10 m+ viewing distance)

When slides are shown on a stage — not viewed on a personal screen — physical distance imposes hard constraints on sizing and contrast.

### Minimum sizes

| Element            | Minimum                          |
|--------------------|----------------------------------|
| Body text          | **2 rem (32 px ≈ 24 pt)**        |
| Slide titles       | **3 rem (48 px ≈ 36 pt)**        |
| Hero / section titles | **5 rem (80 px ≈ 60 pt)**     |
| Labels and captions | **1.5 rem (24 px ≈ 18 pt)**    |
| Absolute floor     | **1.25 rem (20 px)** — nothing below this |

Projectors compress contrast. Use a minimum **7:1** contrast ratio (not just WCAG AA 4.5:1) for all text.

Use **medium (500) or semibold (600)** weight for body text; **bold (700–800)** for headings. Thin and light weights disappear beyond 8 m.

### Density limits at stage sizes

| Element type       | Max count |
|--------------------|-----------|
| Bullet points      | 3–4       |
| Table rows         | 4–5       |
| Table columns      | 3         |
| Parallel cards     | 2         |

If content exceeds these limits, split into multiple slides (Rule 1).

### Stage checklist

- [ ] Body text ≥ 2 rem (32 px)
- [ ] Slide titles ≥ 3 rem (48 px)
- [ ] No text below 1.5 rem (24 px) — including labels, captions, footnotes
- [ ] Font weight: medium or bold only — no thin or light
- [ ] Contrast ≥ 7:1 for all text
- [ ] Max 3–4 bullet points per slide
- [ ] Every element passes a "thumbnail test": legible at 200 × 113 px preview
